How JADE Works

Jadhav Adaptive Daily Equity — a daily competitive rating that measures how every NSE-listed equity has performed against the entire universe of peers, across multiple time horizons, every trading day for years.

The daily competition

Every trading day after market close, every eligible NSE stock enters a competition against the rest of the market. The competition runs in three parallel categories — each measuring a different time horizon — plus a weighted Combined rating.

Long Horizon
Compounder
Measures sustained multi-year performance. Built from 1-year, 2-year, 3-year, and 5-year price progress. The category that identifies durable long-term performers.
Mid Horizon
Momentum
Measures medium-term trends. Built from 3-month, 6-month, 9-month, 1-year, and 2-year price moves. Captures stocks in active multi-quarter uptrends.
Short Horizon
Breakout
Measures recent short-term strength. Built from 1-week, 2-week, 1-month, and 3-month price action. The category that flags new breakouts and momentum bursts.

How JADE points are earned and lost

Each daily competition pairs every stock against other stocks of similar current rating, and decides the outcome based on each stock's category score for that day. The category score itself is built from how the stock's returns rank against every other stock at each of the timeframes that category uses.

Stocks that perform well earn JADE points from the stocks they outscore. The size of the gain depends on the gap between the two stocks' pre-competition ratings — beating a much higher-rated stock earns more JADE than beating a similar one. This is exactly how competitive ratings work in sports leagues and ranking systems: upsets matter more than expected wins, which means a previously-unknown stock that breaks out can climb fast, and a high-rated stock that starts losing falls fast.

This produces three properties that make JADE useful:

  • It accumulates evidence. A stock's rating reflects years of daily competitions, not just yesterday's price. Brief noise gets smoothed out; durable strength builds up.
  • It's relative, not absolute. A JADE of 2000 means "consistently beats the universe", not "is up X% in absolute terms". Bull and bear markets don't inflate or deflate the system.
  • It self-corrects. When a stock stops performing, it loses matches and its rating falls. There's no permanent enthronement — the Hall of Fame page tracks how often #1 changes hands.

Trophies and Cups

Daily ratings produce three layers of achievement:

  • Monthly Trophy. In each category, the stock with the most days at #1 during a calendar month earns that month's trophy.
  • Yearly Cup. The stock with the most Monthly Trophies in a category during a calendar year wins that category's Yearly Cup.
  • World Cup. The rarest prize. A stock wins the World Cup by claiming the Yearly Cup in all three categories (Compounder, Momentum, AND Breakout) in the same calendar year. Equivalent to dominating short, medium, and long horizons simultaneously — the universe-best stock for that year.

Visit the Hall of Fame page to see the World Cup standings, the Monthly Trophy roll, and which stock currently sits at #1 in each category.

How to read a stock's JADE

  • Higher = consistently strong. A stock at 2500 has competed well, day after day, for a long time. A stock at 1000 is around the starting line. A stock at 500 has been losing competitions consistently.
  • Look at all four ratings together. A stock can be high in Breakout (recent breakout) while low in Compounder (no long track record). The full picture comes from all four.
  • Combined JADE is the headline. A 50% / 30% / 20% blend of Compounder / Momentum / Breakout. For most investors, this is the single number to watch.
  • Check the rank, not just the number. A 2200 rating in a universe where the top stock is 3300 ranks 50th. Same 2200 in a quieter universe might be #5. The leaderboard tells you the rank explicitly.

Why ~1,249 stocks and not all ~1,909?

NSE lists roughly 1,909 equity tickers, but most of them are not what you'd actually invest in. JADE deliberately filters the universe down to 1,249 high-quality, tradeable stocks so the rankings reflect real competitive performance — not data noise.

Here is the exact funnel:

Filter step Stocks remaining Why this filter?
Start — all NSE listings 1,909 Every ticker with data from NSE bhavcopies.
– ETFs / index funds / commodity ETFs (163) 1,746 JADE rates individual equities. Gold/silver ETFs, NIFTYBEES, sector ETFs etc. aren't companies — they're fund products tracking an index or commodity.
– Suspended or no-price stocks (217) 1,529 Tickers without a current closing price are either suspended, delisted, or in some other corporate event state — not tradeable today.
– Penny stocks below ₹50 (0) 1,529 At very low prices, a 50-paisa move looks like a 10% return. This makes returns noisy and the resulting JADE ratings unreliable. ₹50 is the floor where the ratings start to mean something.
– Illiquid (<₹1Cr daily turnover) (280) 1,249 If a stock's 90-day average daily turnover is under ₹1Cr, you can't realistically buy or sell more than a few lakh rupees worth without moving the price yourself. We exclude these so JADE rankings reflect stocks you can actually trade at the shown prices.

For context: the popular Nifty 500 benchmark includes only ~500 stocks. JADE's filtered universe is about 2.5× larger than Nifty 500 — we keep many mid- and small-cap names that have real liquidity and tradeable prices but aren't yet in the Nifty 500. That's where most of the discovery opportunities live.

Sector and cap classification

Once a stock is in the universe, we attach a sector and market-cap tag:

  • Sectors — a hybrid of NSE's official Industry classification (covers the broad strokes) plus a curated list providing finer subdivisions where useful (Banks split Private vs PSU; Financial Services split into NBFC / AMC / Insurance / Capital Markets; etc.).
  • Market cap — Nifty 100 = Large; Nifty Midcap 150 = Mid; Nifty Smallcap 250 = Small; everything outside the Nifty 500 = Micro.

What JADE is not

  • Not investment advice. JADE is a quantitative ranking — what you do with it is your call.
  • Not a price target or return forecast. A high JADE reflects competitive history; it makes no claim about tomorrow's price.
  • Not a fundamental rating. JADE doesn't see valuations, balance sheets, management quality, governance, or news. It is purely a competitive-performance rating derived from price history.
  • Not static. JADE changes every trading day. Today's #1 may not be next week's #1 — the system is designed to surface live competitive form, not lock in past glories.

Past performance does not predict future results. Position sizing, diversification, and risk management remain the responsibility of the investor.
